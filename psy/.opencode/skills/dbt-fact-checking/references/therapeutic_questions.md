# Therapeutic Questioning Techniques

This reference provides examples of effective therapeutic questions organized by purpose and step in the fact-checking process.

## Principles of Therapeutic Questioning

### Socratic Method
- Ask questions that guide discovery rather than provide answers
- Build on the user's own observations and insights
- Encourage critical thinking about automatic thoughts

### Open-Ended Questions
- Avoid yes/no questions when exploring
- Use "what," "how," and "tell me more" to encourage elaboration
- Example: "What went through your mind?" vs. "Did you feel bad?"

### Gentle Challenge
- Question assumptions without invalidating feelings
- Use curiosity rather than confrontation
- Example: "I'm curious - what makes you certain about that?"

## Questions by Process Step

### Step 1: Describing the Situation Objectively

**Separating facts from interpretations:**
- "What actually happened, without any interpretation?"
- "If someone recorded this on video, what would they see and hear?"
- "Can you describe this as if you were a neutral observer?"

**Getting specific details:**
- "What exactly did they say/do?"
- "When did this happen?"
- "Who was present?"
- "Where were you when this occurred?"

**Redirecting from judgments:**
- "When you say 'rude,' what specifically did you observe?"
- "Let's pause there - 'they were being difficult' is an interpretation. What did you actually see?"
- "What's the observable behavior behind that conclusion?"

---

### Step 2: Identifying and Rating Emotions

**Naming emotions:**
- "What are you feeling right now as you talk about this?"
- "How would you label this emotion?"
- "Are there multiple emotions mixed together?"
- "Is this more like anger, sadness, fear, or something else?"

**Distinguishing similar emotions:**
- "Does this feel more like anger or frustration?"
- "Is this anxiety about the future or sadness about the present?"
- "Is this guilt about what you did, or shame about who you are?"

**Rating intensity:**
- "On a scale from 0 to 10, where 0 is calm and 10 is the most [emotion] you've ever felt, where are you right now?"
- "If 10 is the most intense this emotion could possibly be, where would you rate this?"
- "Has it been higher or lower than this recently?"

**Exploring nuances:**
- "Where do you feel this emotion in your body?"
- "When did you first notice this feeling?"
- "Does it come in waves or is it constant?"

---

### Step 3: Identifying Interpretations and Thoughts

**Uncovering automatic thoughts:**
- "What goes through your mind when you think about this situation?"
- "What does this mean to you?"
- "What's your explanation for why this happened?"
- "What are you telling yourself about this?"

**Exploring predictions:**
- "What do you think will happen next?"
- "What's the worst-case scenario you're imagining?"
- "What consequences are you worried about?"

**Identifying mind-reading:**
- "What do you think they were thinking?"
- "How do you know that's what they meant?"
- "What makes you believe that's their motivation?"

**Noticing patterns:**
- "Have you had this thought before in other situations?"
- "Does this remind you of anything?"
- "Is this a familiar feeling?"

**Gentle challenges to distortions:**
- "When you say 'always' or 'never,' is that literally true?"
- "You're predicting the future - how certain can you be?"
- "You're reading their mind - but have they actually said this?"

---

### Step 4: Checking the Evidence

**Examining supporting evidence:**
- "What facts support this thought?"
- "What have you observed that makes you think this?"
- "What actually happened that fits with this interpretation?"

**Examining contradicting evidence:**
- "What evidence goes against this thought?"
- "Have there been times when the opposite was true?"
- "What doesn't fit with this conclusion?"

**Considering alternatives:**
- "What are other possible explanations?"
- "If you were wrong about this, what might be true instead?"
- "What would your best friend say about this situation?"
- "How might someone else interpret the same events?"

**Gaining perspective:**
- "If your friend came to you with this exact situation, what would you tell them?"
- "Looking at this from the outside, what do you notice?"
- "What would a neutral observer conclude?"
- "Is there information you don't have that could change your view?"

**Reality testing:**
- "Has this thought been accurate in the past?"
- "When you've thought this before, what actually happened?"
- "What's the actual evidence vs. what you're assuming?"

**Identifying unknowns:**
- "What don't you know for certain?"
- "What are you assuming vs. what do you actually know?"
- "What questions would need to be answered to know for sure?"

---

### Step 5: Reassessing Emotion Intensity

**Re-rating:**
- "Now that we've looked at the evidence, where would you rate this emotion on that 0-10 scale?"
- "Has the intensity shifted at all?"
- "Does this emotion feel the same, stronger, or weaker than before?"

**Exploring the shift (or lack thereof):**
- "What changed in how you're seeing this?"
- "Why do you think the feeling has [increased/decreased/stayed the same]?"
- "What new understanding affected the intensity?"

**Evaluating fit:**
- "Does this level of emotion match what the facts support?"
- "Is this intensity proportional to the actual situation?"
- "If you could adjust the emotion to fit the facts, what would that feel like?"

**Acknowledging persistent emotions:**
- "Even though the facts don't fully support it, the emotion is still strong. What do you make of that?"
- "What else might be contributing to this feeling?"
- "Could there be something from your past affecting this?"

---

### Step 6: Choosing Appropriate Action

**Exploring options:**
- "Given what we've discovered, what options do you see?"
- "What feels like the most helpful response right now?"
- "What would align with your values in this situation?"

**Action vs. acceptance:**
- "Is there something you need to do, or is this more about accepting reality?"
- "What's in your control here?"
- "Would taking action help, or would accepting and letting go serve you better?"

**Opposite action (when emotion doesn't fit facts):**
- "If the emotion doesn't fit the facts, what would acting opposite to it look like?"
- "What would you do if you felt differently?"
- "How might you behave if this emotion weren't driving you?"

**Identifying next steps:**
- "What's one small step you could take?"
- "What would be most helpful right now?"
- "How do you want to move forward with this new understanding?"

**Values alignment:**
- "What matters most to you in this situation?"
- "What kind of person do you want to be here?"
- "What would your best self do?"

---

## Validation Statements

Use these throughout the process to maintain warmth and support:

- "That makes sense given what you're experiencing"
- "I can understand why you'd feel that way"
- "That sounds really difficult"
- "It takes courage to look at this so honestly"
- "You're doing important work right now"
- "That's a really insightful observation"
- "I appreciate you sharing that with me"
- "It's okay to feel [emotion], and it's also okay to question the thoughts driving it"

---

## When to Pause and Check In

- "How are you doing with this process?"
- "Does this make sense so far?"
- "Should we slow down or take a break?"
- "What's coming up for you right now?"
- "Is this feeling too overwhelming, or are you okay to continue?"

---

## Handling Resistance or Difficulty

- "I notice you're hesitating - what's making this hard?"
- "It's okay if you're not sure. Let's explore together"
- "This can be uncomfortable - would it help to take a different angle?"
- "What would make this easier for you?"
- "You don't have to have all the answers right now"
