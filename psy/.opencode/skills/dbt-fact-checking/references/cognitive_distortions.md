# Cognitive Distortions Reference Guide

This reference provides detailed information about common cognitive distortions to help identify thinking patterns that may not match objective reality.

## Common Cognitive Distortions

### 1. Catastrophizing (Fortune Telling)

**Definition:** Predicting the worst possible outcome without evidence.

**Examples:**
- "If I fail this test, I'll never get into college"
- "They haven't texted back - they must hate me"
- "This headache means I have a brain tumor"

**Questions to ask:**
- "What's the worst that could realistically happen?"
- "What are other possible outcomes?"
- "Have you survived similar situations before?"

---

### 2. Mind Reading

**Definition:** Assuming you know what others are thinking without evidence.

**Examples:**
- "She thinks I'm stupid"
- "Everyone at the party thought I was boring"
- "My boss is disappointed in me"

**Questions to ask:**
- "What evidence do you have for what they're thinking?"
- "Could there be other explanations for their behavior?"
- "Have you asked them directly?"

---

### 3. Black-and-White Thinking (All-or-Nothing)

**Definition:** Seeing things in extremes with no middle ground.

**Examples:**
- "If I'm not perfect, I'm a total failure"
- "Either they love me or hate me"
- "I always mess everything up" or "I never do anything right"

**Questions to ask:**
- "What would be between these two extremes?"
- "Is there any gray area here?"
- "Can something be partially true?"

---

### 4. Overgeneralization

**Definition:** Making broad conclusions based on limited evidence.

**Examples:**
- "I failed once, so I'll always fail"
- "Nobody likes me" (based on one rejection)
- "This always happens to me"

**Questions to ask:**
- "Does this really happen every time?"
- "Can you think of exceptions?"
- "What would 'sometimes' or 'occasionally' sound like?"

---

### 5. Personalization

**Definition:** Taking responsibility for things outside your control or assuming events relate to you.

**Examples:**
- "My friend is upset, so I must have done something wrong"
- "The project failed because of me" (when many factors contributed)
- "They're laughing - they must be laughing at me"

**Questions to ask:**
- "What else could explain this situation?"
- "What factors were outside your control?"
- "Could this have nothing to do with you?"

---

### 6. Emotional Reasoning

**Definition:** Believing that feelings reflect objective reality.

**Examples:**
- "I feel stupid, therefore I am stupid"
- "I feel anxious, so something bad will happen"
- "I feel like a burden, so I must be one"

**Questions to ask:**
- "What's the difference between feeling this way and it being true?"
- "What evidence exists beyond the feeling?"
- "Have your feelings been wrong before?"

---

### 7. Should Statements

**Definition:** Using rigid "should," "must," or "ought" statements that create pressure.

**Examples:**
- "I should be over this by now"
- "People should always be fair"
- "I must never make mistakes"

**Questions to ask:**
- "Who says it 'should' be this way?"
- "What if you replaced 'should' with 'prefer' or 'wish'?"
- "Is this expectation realistic?"

---

### 8. Labeling

**Definition:** Attaching fixed, global labels to yourself or others.

**Examples:**
- "I'm a loser"
- "She's an idiot"
- "I'm unlovable"

**Questions to ask:**
- "Is one behavior really your entire identity?"
- "What's a more specific, less judgmental description?"
- "Would you use this label on a friend in the same situation?"

---

### 9. Discounting the Positive

**Definition:** Rejecting or minimizing positive experiences or qualities.

**Examples:**
- "That success doesn't count"
- "They're just being nice"
- "Anyone could have done that"

**Questions to ask:**
- "Why doesn't this count?"
- "Would you dismiss this if it happened to a friend?"
- "What would it mean if you accepted this positive?"

---

### 10. Magnification/Minimization

**Definition:** Exaggerating negatives or diminishing positives.

**Examples:**
- "This tiny mistake ruined everything" (magnification)
- "Sure I got the promotion, but it's not a big deal" (minimization)

**Questions to ask:**
- "On a scale of 1-10, how significant is this really?"
- "Will this matter in a week? A year?"
- "What's a more balanced perspective?"

---

## Using This Reference

When identifying potential cognitive distortions:

1. **Be gentle and curious** - Never use these labels to criticize the user
2. **Frame as possibilities** - "I wonder if this might be catastrophizing?" vs. "You're catastrophizing"
3. **Ask, don't tell** - Guide the user to recognize patterns themselves
4. **Validate first** - Acknowledge why the thought makes sense before questioning it
5. **Use Socratic questions** - Help users discover alternative perspectives

## Integration with Fact-Checking

These distortions often appear in **Step 3** (Identify Interpretations) and are examined in **Step 4** (Check the Evidence). Use this reference to:

- Recognize patterns in the user's thinking
- Formulate appropriate questions
- Suggest alternative perspectives gently
- Help users see gaps between thoughts and facts
